Exploring underlying pore-scale dynamics with pressure data spectra: Impact of cyclic CO₂ injection on residual trapping

Introduction

The storage of CO₂ in underground saline aquifers will be integral in reducing anthropogenic emissions, and meeting the targets laid out by the 2015 Paris agreement [1], [2]. Finding ways to improve trapping in the subsurface, and minimizing CO₂ plume spread will be important in CCS (carbon capture and storage) projects as this improves storage security and reduces costs [3].

Cyclic injection (where repeated cycles of CO₂ and water are injected into the subsurface) may improve residual trapping, which will improve the storage security of CO₂ [4]. As a result, it has received interest from members of the scientific community [5], [6], [7]. With repeated cycles of alternating CO₂ and water injection, the CO₂ has been observed to break apart into smaller clusters that are trapped in the pore space of the rock [6].

Imaging experiments that allow us to observe residually trapped CO₂ in the pore space are time consuming, expensive, and limit sample size [8], [9]. In contrast, if we are able to infer the underlying pore-scale dynamics from pressure data, then we will be able to collect more data on more samples in a given time frame, and explore residually trapping in larger cores, with different heterogeneities.

In this work, we highlight how underlying pore-scale dynamics can be inferred from the spectral power of pressure measurements taken during core-scale experiments using continuous wavelet transformation (CWT). Using the method described in [10], we explore pressure data collected by [5], and compare it to pore-scale observations made by [6]. We highlight the importance of the evolution of the spectral signal in the pressure data, and emphasise that information about fluid dynamical evolution of flow in core is lost in averaging the pressure data in time.

Core-scale Results

The core-scale experiment assessed in this paper was conducted by [5] on a cylindrical Fell sandstone sample, 38 mm in diameter and 80 mm in length. In total, 6 cycles of water injection, followed by scCO₂ (super critical CO₂) injection, were performed, with the flow rate kept constant at 1 ml/min. The pressure data from this experiment is shown in Figure 1. Edlmann et al. (2019) concluded that cyclic injection promoted residual trapping, shown by the differential pressure increasing with cycle number (most notable between panels 2 and 3 in Figure 1, i.e., the difference between differential pressure during water injection during cycle 1 and cycle 2). However, the underlying pore-scale dynamics that cause the increase in trapping were not known.

We can use continuous wavelet transformation to explore the underlying pore-scale dynamics Spurin et al., 2023. The spectral power of the pressure data for the full experiment is shown in Figure 2a. The pressure data was then split into periods of CO2 injection and periods of water injection (identified by Edlmann et al., 2019 and indicated by the panels in Figure 1). The spectral power for the split pressure signal is shown in Figure 2b for the periods of CO2 injection and Figure 2c for the periods of water injection.

The spectral power for the full pressure signal in Figure 2a shows some variations, but no clear trend is observable. Whereas, the spectral power for solely periods of CO2 injection (Figure 2b) indicates that the longer periods (> 300 s) gain power with increasing cycle number. Conversely, the spectral power for solely water injection indicates decreasing power with increasing cycle number for these longer periods. Thus, when combined, the evolution during water injection counters the signal during CO2 injection, making it more difficult to draw conclusions from the data.

The time-averaged wavelet power spectra is shown in Figure 3. This is equivalent to the Fourier transform of the pressure data. The calculated spectra are broadly consistent with red noise for both CO2 injection and water injection, similar to other pore-scale observations Spurin et al., 2022. The evolution from early cycles to later cycles is subtle, with shorter frequencies (longer periods) decreasing in amplitude for water injection, and increasing for CO2 injection (same observations as Figure 2b–c). This highlights that the evolution in connectivity due to cyclic injection is subtle.

In previous work, event frequency during steady-state flow correlated to event size, with larger events occurring over longer periods Spurin et al., 2022. Assuming the same dynamics in these experiments (as we also observe red noise), the increase in power at the longer timescales during CO2 injection suggests that larger events are becoming more important in determining pressure fluctuations. We hypothesize this would be related to more gas in the system, or larger ganglia of gas becoming mobile.

Conversely, the decrease in spectral power during cyclic water injection suggests that larger events are becoming less important. This could be smaller ganglia forming, or water being forced out the core. These observations agree with the conclusions reached by Edlmann et al., 2019, where cyclic injection led to more CO2 trapping.

Comparison to Pore-scale Results

The pore-scale experiment was conducted on a cylindrical Bentheimer sandstone sample, 6.1 mm in diameter and ~40 mm in length. This sample is approximately 1% of the total volume of the core-scale experiment. For this experiment, the fluid interfaces could be resolved directly. A full description is provided in Herring et al., 2016.

This experiment showed that, with increasing cycle number, the gas saturation and trapping efficiency both increased, i.e., more gas was present, and the amount of gas trapped as a proportion of the total gas increased. They observed that the average size of gas ganglion increased, but the overall connectivity decreased.

The initial gas saturation increases between the first and second cycle of CO2. This difference is less pronounced between the second and third cycle. The average size of gas ganglia and the total amount of gas increase with cycle number. This observation was also seen in the spectral power of the pressure fluctuations in the core-scale results, where pore-scale imaging is not possible.

Conclusions and Outlook

In this work the underlying pore scale dynamis were inferred from the pressure data for core-scale experiments. When compared to the pore-scale results (where dynamics may have change, due to scale), we see that our inferences are supported. Overall, this is a demonstration that the spectral power of the pressure signal can be used to understand the evolution of trapping in the system. Using continuous wavelet transformations to explore the spectral content of the pressure data is useful in assessing the role of cyclic CO$_2$ injection in improving residual trapping. Future work exploring the presence of this signal in other cores, and at the field scale would be of interest. To have the pressure data for pore-scale observations, and match it to the dynamics in the pore-scale would be of use for future upscaling work.
